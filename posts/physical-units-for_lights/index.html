Physical Units For Lights

02-03-2021 — Written by François Guthmann — 9 min read

Already read the article but need a reminder? Go to the TL;DR right now!

I have been willing to create this blog post for a while now. Every time I need to work with lights and physical units I forget which one is which. Fortunately there are lots of great resources out there already making the connection between the quantities and units or even optic and electromagnetic quantities. The incredible Filament documentation, itself inspired by Moving Frostbite to PBR are great places to look for a different take on this.

One of the things that I find sometimes missing out there is an explanation in plain english of what those quantities are. If I can’t visualize something or put words on it it’s hard for me to grasp. I’m hoping to bridge that gap here on top of providing a very concise and efficient reminder.

It’s all about energy

There many advantages to using physical units for lights, amongst which are the ability to compare with real life measurements, to ensure consistency across rendering engines and even to easily model real world lamps. In the context of Physically Based Rendering, where Energy Conservation And Reciprocity are key, it is pretty much necessary.

In order to understand what the different units are and how they are related to each other we’ll have to start from the bottom of the pyramid and build our way up from there. And if you think about it, light itself is fundamentally energy. Energy can be expressed in joule, a unit derived from the SI System.

Now what we casually call ‘lights’ in our renderers are really light sources. They are generating light constantly. It means that by one way or another they are emitting energy per unit of time. This is usually called a power in physics. If we want to express our energy in joule (j) per unit of time in seconds we get watts. This is called the Radiant flux or Radiant power in the radiometry terminology.

This is a great start, we have found a way to define the energy emitted by any light per unit of time. This sounds like a solid base for our Pyramid. However, it is more common in the computer graphics community to use photometry language rather than radiometry language. Instead of using the Radiant flux or Radiant power, we’ll use the Luminous flux or Luminous power. The difference is that the ‘Luminous’ power of an electromagnetic radiation is weighted based on the sensitivity of the human eye. It kind of makes sense, a radiator emits a lot of Radiant Power in the infrared wavelength but we can’t see that. Measuring of much energy a lightbulb emits with this metric would be useless to determine how bright it is to a human.

The conversion between the true power and the luminous power is defined by the Luminous Efficacy function. This functions basically defines how efficiently a lightsource actually lights, ie how much of the energy it emits is actually visible by the human eye. Because we use this function to convert from Radiant Power to Luminous Power, the latter cannot be homogeneous to a power. That’s fine, we’ll use our own unit, with black body and HOOKERS PUN. Thus was born the lumen (lm). Unfortunately in the SI system, the lumen is a derived unit and not the main one. I find this weird and confusing but whatever, we’ll catch up with the SI system later on.

It’s important to understand that the Luminous Power still behaves like a power. Effectively it IS a power, just measured with a crappy sensor. In your head, you can still think about it as a measure of energy per unit of time even if this isn’t 100% accurate.

Note that if you buy lightbulbs these days, the intensity of the light is probably advertised in lumen. This makes it a very convenient unit to use when writing the tools artists will use to author light sources. However, if you’ve looked at some engine documentations or even the links I provided at the beginning you might have encountered the lux (lx) and candela (cd) units. The lux in particular is often used to describe the sun intensity and IES profiles store candelas.

Direction oriented design

Let’s talk about the candela unit first, which is the Luminous Intensity quantity. It is the base unit of all the other Luminous quantities in the SI system. One of its definition is lumen per steradian. This unit measures a power-like quantity in a single infinitesimal direction.

I know the per something terminology can be confusing but it always means the same thing: for an infinitely small something. Let’s say we have a punctual lightsource that emits 1 candela for every possible direction. This means no matter what infinitesimal direction you choose, if you measure the Luminous Intensity you get 1 candela. If we want to know how much energy is emitted in the upper hemisphere, we basically want to know how much energy is emitted in half the possible directions, so 0.5 * 4 PI = 2 PI. Well if our lightsource emits 1 candela, so 1 lumen / steradian and we are looking at a solid angle of 2 PI, this means we emit 2 PI lumens in that part of the world.

It is also important to understand the opposite way of seeing things which can sometimes be counter intuitive. While you can specify how many lumens there are in a finite solid angle, you cannot measure how many lumens there are in an infinitely small direction. For a specific direction, one can only measure an amount of candela.

Before we jump to the next unit we still need to be clear on one last thing. We only spoke about a punctual light source so far, but everything we said is also true for an area light. If we think of a square area light and measure that it emits 1 candela in a specific direction, it means the entire area light contributed. By that I mean that even though we measured a single direction, every single point on the surface contributed to that specific direction. It might seem obvious now, but it will be important later.

Space oriented design

Let’s talk about the lux unit now. It is the unit describing the Luminous Emittance or Illuminance quantity. The lux unit is defined as lumen per square meter. It measures a power-like quantity leaving a single infinitesimal area.

Let’s follow the same kind of example as above and chose a square light of size 2m per side and which surface emits 1 lux. This means every single point on the surface of the square emits 1 lux. If we want to know how much energy is emitted by half the surface of the square, we need to multiply our Luminous Emittance by that area. Our whole square has an area of 2 * 2 = 4 m², so half of the surface, 2m², emits 2 m² * 1 lm / m² = 2 lumen. Somehow, I find this unit easier to understand than the candela.

We can again try to see things the other way. It is possible to measure an amount of lumen emitted by a finite area but it not possible to do the same for a single point in space, ie an infinitely small area. One can only measure the Luminous Emittance in lux of single point in space.

I think no one here was confused by the fact that whenever we mentionned the amount of energy emitted by a single point in space, I meant in no specific direction. Every point on the surface of our square, emits 1 lux of Luminous Emittance and this quantity is spread equally over every possible direction. Again, this might seem obvious but our next unit will combine the concepts of lux and candela in a single master unit and you will probably want to read that again.
